## Structural commitment

Does the draft have a clear, intentional spine — a sequence of distinct argumentative moves that earn their position? Or does it drift between local moves with no organizing structure?

Look for:
- The opening's structural cues (enumerated lists, "three reasons", trailing colon promising an enumeration, posed questions). Is the draft honoring them?
- Section-to-section transitions. Are sections distinct, or does one section paraphrase the next?
- A discernible thesis the draft is building toward. Or a meandering accumulation.
- Sections that could be cut without losing the argument (those are filler).

If the draft has a clean spine, say so in one line and move on. If it doesn't, name the missing/broken structural element specifically — quote the section header (or absence of one) and the passage where the structure breaks down.

## Length fit

Length status is in the user message. Critique symmetrically:

- If draft is **>10% over target**: identify specific passages to cut. Quote the cuttable phrases or paragraphs. Cuts beat compressions; identify what's redundant or under-developed-but-padded rather than asking for "tightening" abstractly. Use this format for cuts:

      Cut: "<verbatim phrase or sentence from the draft>" — Reason: <one-sentence why>

- If draft is **>10% under target**: identify specific argumentative gaps where expansion would land — what example, what counterargument, what consequence is missing? Quote the section that should expand and name what's missing. Long-form essays (alignment / philosophy / strategy register) often run 4k–10k+ words; don't accept a thin draft just because it ends cleanly.

- If draft is **within ±10%**: surface only 1-2 highest-impact issues; don't manufacture a punch list when length is fine. Flag specific quotable filler if any; otherwise note the length is on-target and move on.

Late-round editor over-correction has been observed when critics produce expansion-only or cut-only suggestions against a near-target draft. Be honest about which direction the draft needs.

## Concreteness and citation

Does the draft cite specific cases, names, numbers, papers, dates? Or is it abstract throughout? Concrete content is load-bearing in essays of this register — readers anchor on Tay/Sydney/Haugen/Myanmar-style paradigm cases, on quantitative claims with sources, on dated events.

Look for:
- Every section that makes a structural claim — does it support that claim with at least one concrete example, dated event, or named referent? Or just abstract framings?
- Phrases like "studies show", "many people argue", "it's been demonstrated that" — vague-attribution tells. Quote them.
- The draft naming a category (e.g. "AI safety incidents") without naming any specific case.
- Numbers and dates: the draft makes a quantitative claim (e.g. "frequently", "often") that could be sharpened to a specific figure or range.

Don't ask the writer to fabricate sources. Do flag the *opportunities* where a concrete case, citation, or figure would tighten the prose.

## Voice and argumentative thread

Does the draft preserve the opening's voice — tone, register, sentence rhythm, vocabulary set? Does it advance the opening's argument or restate it?

Look for:
- Drift from the opening's voice (e.g. opening is acerbic and informal, draft is hedged and formal).
- Restatement of the opening's claims rather than advancement.
- Generic AI-essay tics: tricolons that build to nothing, "this raises questions", "in some sense", parallel structure used as decoration, performative hedging ("it is important to note", "while it is true that...").
- Sections where the draft pivots away from the opening's actual argument toward a generic adjacent topic.

Quote the offending passages. Quote the opening for comparison where useful.
