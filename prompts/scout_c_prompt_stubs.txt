scout_c_how_true. You are given a claim, along with any existing "how true" stories that have already been identified. Your task is to scout for additional plausible causal stories for why this claim might be true that aren't already covered. For each story: what is the mechanism? What is actually going on in the world that makes the claim true? What observable consequences would we expect if this mechanism is operating? Be as specific and concrete as you can about the causal chain. Focus on stories that are genuinely different from ones already identified, not minor variations.


scout_c_how_false. You are given a claim, along with any existing "how true" and "how false" stories. Your task is to scout for additional plausible causal stories about how the world works that are compatible with the evidence we've observed but in which the claim is false. These aren't about poking holes in the evidence—they're concrete alternative pictures of what might actually be going on, where the same observations hold but the claim doesn't. Focus on stories that are genuinely different from ones already identified and that are plausible enough to be worth taking seriously.


scout_c_cruxes. You are given a claim along with the existing "how true" and "how false" stories. Your task is to scout for cruxes not yet identified—specific points where the stories diverge, such that resolving them would tell you which story is closer to the truth. A crux may be a claim (something whose truth or falsity is load-bearing) or a question (something whose answer would discriminate between stories). For each crux: what exactly is the disagreement between the stories on this point? What would you expect to observe under each story? How feasible is it to investigate? Rank by a combination of importance and tractability.


scout_c_relevant_evidence. You are given a claim along with its "how true" and "how false" stories and identified cruxes. Your task is to scout for questions about evidence that, if answered, would bear on the most important cruxes. Frame each as a question: "what does the literature say about X?", "what is the actual rate of Y?", "are there documented cases of Z?" For each: which crux does it bear on? Which story would be favored by which answer? How concrete and answerable is this question? Prioritize questions that would discriminate between stories over questions whose answers would merely be consistent with one.


scout_c_stress_test_cases. You are given a claim along with its "how true" and "how false" stories. Your task is to scout for concrete specific scenarios that could serve as stress-tests for the claim—especially boundary cases where the different stories would predict different outcomes. Frame each as a question: "what does [scenario] tell us about [the claim]?" For each candidate: briefly describe the scenario, explain why it would be a good test (what makes it hard for the claim to pass), and note which stories it would help discriminate between. Don't perform the full analysis of each case—just identify promising cases for further investigation.
