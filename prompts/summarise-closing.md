Now it's your turn to write the executive summary. 



A suggested format:

1. Question 

Restate the question clearly in your own words.

2\. Bottom line

State the overall assessment in 2–4 sentences. What is the answer, and how confident should the reader be? This should be the clearest, most direct thing in the report.

3\. Key considerations

Present the strongest considerations impacting on the question, regardless of what direction they point. For each one, don't just name it — unpack it enough that the reader understands why it matters. Aim for 2–5 core considerations.

4\. Further considerations

Briefly list further considerations that you think are worth being aware with -- with a few words about each one explaining why it isn't more crucial (if that isn't obvious).

5\. Sensitivity analysis

Identify the 1–3 things the overall assessment most depends on. What are the key uncertainties? What would change the conclusion if it turned out differently? 

6\. A brief honest note on how much weight to put on this assessment. What are the limitations of the research?



Some tips:

\- Write for a reader who is coming to this fresh.
- Avoid rhetorical flourishes. Keep sentences short and to the point.

\- Write for a reader who is highly intelligent but easily bored.

\- Prefer concrete over abstract. Prefer specific over vague.

\- Don't repeat yourself across sections.

\- Do not refer to anything about the research analysis. You are discussing the \*answer to the question\*, not the process that led to this. The only exception to this is when discussing confidence, when you may mention it if helpful (but it is not necessary)

\- The most important thing to convey are the considerations (so that the reader can form their own view), rather than an overall judgement. But your judgements will be important in helping the reader to orient, know which considerations to give most attention to, etc.



Be calm. Do not feel a need to be small, and do not shy away from offering opinions if those are what you feel.
