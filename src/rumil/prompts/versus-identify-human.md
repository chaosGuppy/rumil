# Versus: Identify the human continuation

You are looking at two continuations of the same essay opening. **Exactly one was written by the human author of the essay; the other was generated by a language model.** Your job is to identify which is which.

This is a discrimination task, not a preference task — quality is not the question. A continuation can be the better-written one and still be the language model's, or the rougher one and still be the human's.

## What to attend to

Possibly useful signals (none individually decisive):

- **Voice consistency with the opening.** Does the continuation pick up the author's specific cadence, idiosyncratic word choices, and willingness to be blunt or weird, or does it drift toward a more neutral register?
- **Hedging patterns.** Language models often hedge in characteristic ways — symmetric "on the one hand / on the other", reflexive "it's worth noting that", balancing every claim with a counter-claim. Humans hedge too, but usually less uniformly.
- **Structural tics.** Heavy signposting, exhaustive enumeration, paragraph-level parallelism, tidy three-part lists, and conclusion-restating-introduction structure are more common in model output than in good human essay writing.
- **Concreteness and commitment.** Is the continuation willing to name specific people, papers, numbers, dates, anecdotes, or take a stance the author would have to defend? Or does it stay at a level of abstraction where nothing can be wrong?
- **Local prose texture.** Sentence-length variance, willingness to start a sentence with "And" or "But", em-dashes used for genuine asides vs. as filler, etc.
- **Topical drift.** Models sometimes engage with a generic version of the essay's topic rather than the specific frame the opening set up.

These are heuristics, not rules — humans write generic-sounding paragraphs sometimes, and good models can produce specific, voice-consistent prose. Use judgment.

## How to work

1. Read the opening carefully — the author's voice in the opening is your reference signal.
2. Read both continuations.
3. Note specific passages that move your estimate one way or the other. Quote them.
4. Don't anchor on which is *better* — sometimes the human is rougher.

## Output

Produce a structured analysis:

- **Opening voice**: 1–2 sentences on the author's voice in the opening.
- **A: signals**: specific passages or patterns in A that point toward human or model, with which way they point.
- **B: signals**: same for B.
- **Synthesis**: which way the evidence balances, and how confidently.

End your response with **one of these labels on its own line**, repurposed as a confidence scale for your identification:

- "A strongly preferred" — high confidence A is the human
- "A somewhat preferred" — moderate confidence A is the human
- "A slightly preferred" — slight lean toward A being the human
- "Approximately indifferent between A and B" — cannot tell
- "B slightly preferred" — slight lean toward B being the human
- "B somewhat preferred" — moderate confidence B is the human
- "B strongly preferred" — high confidence B is the human
